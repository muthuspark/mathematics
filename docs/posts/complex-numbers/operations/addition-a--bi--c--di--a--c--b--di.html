Addition of Complex Numbers: A Comprehensive Explanation

In mathematics, complex numbers extend the concept of real numbers by introducing an imaginary component. A complex number is typically expressed as \(a + bi\), where: - \(a\) is the real part, - \(b\) is the coefficient of the imaginary unit \(i\), - and \(i^2 = -1\).

When adding two complex numbers, such as \((a + bi) + (c + di)\), the process involves combining their real parts and their imaginary parts separately. This rule stems from the structure of complex numbers and ensures consistency with other number systems.

Step-by-Step Explanation:

  1. Identify Components: Break down each complex number into its real and imaginary components.
    • First number: \(a\) (real part), \(b\) (coefficient for \(i\))
    • Second number: \(c\) (real part), \(d\) (coefficient for \(i\))
  2. Add Real Parts: Sum the real parts of both numbers.
    • Result: \(a + c\)
  3. Add Imaginary Coefficients: Sum the coefficients of the imaginary units (\(b\) and \(d\)).
    • Result: \(b + d\)
  4. Combine Results: Form a new complex number with the summed real part and the summed imaginary coefficient.
    • Final result: \((a + c) + (b + d)i\)

Why This Rule Works:

  • Structure of Complex Numbers: Complex numbers follow specific rules akin to vectors in 2D space. Adding vectors involves component-wise addition, which is consistent with complex number addition.

  • Generalization: This rule generalizes operations from real numbers and integers, where the imaginary part is implicitly zero.

Applications:

  • Electrical Engineering: Used in calculating total impedance in AC circuits by summing resistances (real parts) and reactances (imaginary parts).
  • Physics: In wave equations or when dealing with vectors, complex numbers provide a natural framework for combining orthogonal components.

In conclusion, the rule \((a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i\) ensures consistency across mathematical systems and real-world applications by treating real and imaginary parts separately. This approach simplifies operations and broadens applicability in various fields.
